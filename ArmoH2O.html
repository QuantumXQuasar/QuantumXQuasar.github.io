ArmoH2O:

This is one of my earlier projects, so don't judge me too hard :) I was still new to programming and engineering.

In 10th grade, one of my friends and I decided to create a cardboard robot for the science fair. We thought about what function the robot would do. Eventually,

we decided to build a robotic arm that would pour water or any liquid from one cup to another, hence the name Armo H20. The hardware consisted of cardboard.

Everything was put together by superglue, a hot glue gun, and tape; the robot would have an arm that would turn and pour the water. The arm would turn with

the utilization of two motors; one motor would turn the arm itself, while the other one would rotate the cup attached to the arm. The motors themselves were

connected and controlled by the system controller, which was coded in simple block language: Snap.

The whole project took about 4 days. My friend and I used to meet at the library and work there for hours, designing, building, and coding. We encountered

one problem where our glue could not attach the arm to the upper part of the arm holder. However, the tape came to the rescue, and everything worked out perfectly.

We displayed our robot at the science fair, and judges were impressed by our innovation. We were awarded the "Best Invention Award," which felt rewarding.

After the science fair, I thought about how ArmoH2O could be improved. Looking back, I noticed it would work better with sensors rather than hardcode since

sensors would allow ArmoH2O to pour the water at different positions, Depending on where the cup would be, rather than just one specific position. Sensors would

act as the eyes of the robot. Moreover, Had we had more advanced materials, we would have built our robot with metals rather than cardboard for the apparent reason

that metal is more durable. I aim to make these developments in the future. Considering our skillset and materials we had back then, I am still proud of our little invention.
